Research

Human Gait Health Monitoring Using Structural Vibration Sensing

Gait health information has been shown to be a critical aspect of overall health, diagnosing neurodegenerative conditions and gait disorders, and is especially important in the context of elderly fall prediction. As a result, frequent measurements of gait health parameters outside of clinical settings can help individuals monitor their overall health and improve their quality of life. In our work, we use footstep-induced floor vibrations to learn about gait health. The primary intuition behind this system is that, when a footstep strikes the floor, it induces an impulsive excitation in the floor stucture, which generates a dynamic response (in the form of structural vibrations). By measuring and monitoring these vibration responses, we can infer information about the footstep itself, and, therefore, about the gait health of the person who generated that footstep. This project is a collaboration between CMUs Department of Civil and Environmental Engineering and Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering.


The high-level research question for this work is: "How can we monitor the gait health of human occupants in non-clinical settings using their footstep-induced structural floor vibrations?" This high-level research question can be divided into several sub-questions as follows:

Multiple Walker Separation: How to separate structurally co-dependent multiple person footstep-induced vibrations?
Typical indoor environments contain multiple simultaneous walkers. In these "multiple walker" scenarios, footstep-induced floor vibration responses are an overlapping mixture of the responses from each person and individual information is difficult to separate. We have addressed this challenge through a sparse-representation-based sequential identification and signal separation approach. By identifying and separating individual responses, our approach enables extraction of individual gait health information in multiple walker scenarios.


Publications: IWSHM 2019

Awards: Best Student Paper Award, Dynamics Subcommittee EMI 2020

Footstep Force Estimation: How to model spatially-varying footstep force to structural vibration relationships?
The relationship between measured floor vibration signals and gait health parameters (e.g., footstep ground reaction force (GRF), balance symmetry, etc.) is affected by spatial variations in the structural characteristics (e.g., damping, stiffness, mass, etc.) of the underlying structure (a.k.a. the structural variation effect). Further, healthy humans tend to walk similarly across different steps, resulting in a narrow range of GRFs for training and failure to capture behavior in the "extreme" regions (i.e., "unbalanced gait"). Collecting training data from these "extreme" regions is difficult because most data sources are persons with gait abnormalities, injuries, and/or elder persons - it is not reasonable to ask ones grandparent to walk 100X down a hallway. As a result, this gait sampling bias results in a model bias towards the middle range of GRFs and erroneous estimation of the "extreme" GRFs. The combination of these two effects make it challenging to estimate gait health parameters from the raw vibration signal alone, and introduces significant modeling errors. We address these challenges first by extracting structural regions to overcome spatially-varying vibration behavior, and then by developing a kernel-based robust regression model to overcome biased training data and enable robust gait health monitoring.


Publications: SPIE 2017, IMAC 2018, IMAC 2019, IPSN 2019

Awards: Best Poster Award, IPSN 2019

Human Occupant Monitoring with Structural Vibration Sensing

Indoor occupant monitoring is an important component of many smart and connected buildings. It can be used for energy/space management, security, and personalized services (i.e., personal assistants, etc.). In this work, we monitor indoor occupants by inferring information about their presence, identity, and location. To achieve this, we leverage structural vibration sensing and develop techniques that fuse physical intuition with machine learning/data processing.


This project is highly interdisciplinary and has resulted in publications in both high impact Civil/Mechanical Engineering Journals, as well as highly-selective peer reviewed conferences in the Electrical Engineering/Computer Science field. Specifically, we have shown how our system accurately tracks/detects indoor occupants ( CPD 2018, IEEE Pervasive Computing, IWSHM 2019, Journal of Engineering Mechanics), uniquely identifies them ( UBICOMP/IMWUT 2017), and localizes them ( Mechanical Systems and Signal Processing (MSSP)).


In addition, this work has received several awards, including best student paper awards (EMI 2018, EMI 2019) and best journal paper award (SHM/NDE Technical Committee, American Society of Mechanical Engineers, 2020).

Enabling Building-Scale Interactive Surfaces

This project involves turning everyday surfaces (e.g., tables, walls, etc.) into interactive/touch surfaces. These surfaces can then be used as a means for input to other smart/connected home IoT devices, or can be used for passive monitoring of indoor occupants. This project has two primary directions: 1) "SurfaceVibe": using surface mounted vibration sensors to create interactive surfaces (IPSN 2017), and 2) "OptiStructures": creating interactive structures with feedback displays by embedding fiber optic cables and Fiber Bragg Grating (FBG) sensors into materials (UBICOMP/IMWUT 2020).


The "OptiStructures" component of this project was recently featured in the press by hackster.io. In addition, a video demonstration of this project can be found here.


Reducing Labeled Data Requirements for Medical Image Analysis Using Active Learning

Deep learning models are becoming increasingly common for automated medical image analysis and aid in diagnosis. However, these models require substantial labeled training data, which is costly, time consuming, and/or impractical to obtain. In this work, we develop a novel approach for reducing the labeled training data requirement though active deep learning. Our approach chooses which training data to label at each training iteration based on maximizing information on the decision boundary. As such, we achieve similar results to using the fully-labeled training data with fewer samples.


Publications: ICMLA 2018, Wire's Data Mining and Knowledge Discovery 2020

Awards: Best Paper Award, ICMLA 2018
